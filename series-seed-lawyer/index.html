What Series Seed counsel covers

A Series Seed financing typically involves a stock purchase agreement, investor rights agreement, right of first refusal and co-sale agreement, and voting agreement — plus the term sheet that precedes them. We draft, review, and negotiate all of it.

Term Sheet Review

Before you sign anything, we walk through the term sheet with you — valuation, option pool, liquidation preference, board composition, and protective provisions. The term sheet sets the baseline for everything that follows.

Stock Purchase Agreement

The core acquisition document. We review or draft representations and warranties, closing conditions, and the mechanics of the share issuance — making sure the company's obligations are accurate and bounded.

Investor Rights Agreement

Information rights, pro-rata rights, registration rights, and any right of first offer on new securities. We negotiate the terms that govern your ongoing relationship with investors through future rounds.

ROFR & Co-Sale Agreement

Governs secondary transfers of founder shares. We structure these provisions to protect founders while giving investors the protections they reasonably expect.

Voting Agreement

Board composition, drag-along obligations, and voting obligations in future transactions. Board seats and drag-along triggers negotiated at this stage shape your exit options years from now.

Cap Table & 409A Coordination

We review your cap table for accuracy before the round closes and coordinate the 409A valuation you'll need before issuing post-round employee options.

Why Series Seed is different from a SAFE or convertible note

SAFEs and convertible notes defer the hard questions — valuation, governance, investor rights — until a future priced round. A Series Seed answers them now. That's both the advantage and the reason it requires more care.

In a Series Seed, investors receive actual preferred stock with defined rights: a liquidation preference that determines who gets paid first at exit, voting rights that may give them a say over future financing decisions, and information rights that require ongoing financial reporting. The board may change composition. Future investors will see this document package as the starting point for their own negotiation.

Getting these terms right at the Series Seed stage is materially easier — and less expensive — than renegotiating unfavorable provisions at Series A when leverage has shifted. We approach Series Seed counsel with that long-term view.
