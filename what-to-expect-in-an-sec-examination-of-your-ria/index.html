What to Expect in an SEC Examination of Your RIA

Rafael Recalde March 25, 2026

If you operate a registered investment adviser, an SEC examination is not a question of if but when. The Division of Examinations (formerly the Office of Compliance Inspections and Examinations) conducts thousands of adviser examinations each year, and newly registered advisers are often examined within their first two years of registration. Understanding what to expect—and how to prepare—can be the difference between a routine process and a regulatory crisis.

Types of Examinations

The SEC conducts several categories of examinations, each with a different scope and trigger.

Routine examinations are the most common. These are risk-based assessments that the Division of Examinations schedules as part of its normal oversight cycle. They are not prompted by any particular allegation of wrongdoing. Newly registered advisers, advisers that have never been examined, and advisers with certain risk characteristics (such as high assets under management or particular business models) are prioritized.

Cause examinations are initiated when the SEC has reason to believe that a violation of the securities laws may have occurred. These may be triggered by investor complaints, tips from whistleblowers, referrals from other regulatory bodies, unusual trading activity, or inconsistencies in Form ADV filings. Cause exams tend to be more focused and more intense than routine exams.

Sweep examinations target a specific issue across multiple firms simultaneously. The SEC uses sweeps to assess industry-wide compliance with particular rules or to evaluate emerging risks. Recent sweep topics have included compliance with the Marketing Rule, private fund adviser fee disclosures, and cybersecurity preparedness. If your firm receives a sweep request, other firms in your peer group likely have as well.

What Triggers an Examination

Routine exams are driven by the Division's risk-based selection model, which considers factors including the firm's size, business activities, client types, compensation structure, and length of time since the last examination. But several factors can elevate a firm's risk profile and accelerate the timeline:

  • Material changes to the firm's Form ADV, particularly changes in business activities, conflicts of interest, or disciplinary history
  • Client complaints or tips submitted to the SEC
  • Performance claims or advertising that appears inconsistent with underlying data
  • Involvement in private fund advisory, particularly where valuation practices may be subjective
  • Use of affiliated broker-dealers or other arrangements that create potential conflicts
  • Custody of client assets, especially where the firm does not use a qualified custodian or claims reliance on an exception

The Examination Process

Initial contact and document request. The exam typically begins with a letter or phone call from the examination staff notifying the firm of the upcoming examination and providing an initial document request list. This list can be extensive—often 50 or more line items—and may include the firm's compliance manual, code of ethics, advisory agreements, marketing materials, trade records, fee calculations, client correspondence, and organizational documents. The timeframe for producing these documents is usually two to four weeks, though the staff may accommodate reasonable requests for extensions on particularly voluminous requests.

On-site or remote fieldwork. Examiners will review the documents produced and then conduct fieldwork, which may take place at the firm's offices or remotely. During this phase, examiners may request additional documents, review electronic records, test trade data against compliance policies, and examine the firm's books and records in detail. Remote examinations have become increasingly common, though the SEC retains the right to conduct on-site visits.

Staff interviews. Examiners will typically interview the firm's chief compliance officer, senior management, portfolio managers, and other personnel. These interviews are not depositions—they are not under oath—but statements made during an examination can be used in subsequent enforcement proceedings. It is critical that personnel are prepared, honest, and precise. If someone does not know the answer to a question, the correct response is to say so and offer to follow up, not to speculate.

Common Focus Areas

Custody. The custody rule under the Advisers Act (Rule 206(4)-2) is one of the most examined and most violated provisions. Examiners will scrutinize whether the firm has custody of client assets, whether it has satisfied the qualified custodian and surprise examination requirements, and whether any exceptions apply. Inadvertent custody—such as authority to deduct fees directly from client accounts or standing letters of authorization—is a frequent finding.

Marketing and advertising. Since the Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1) took effect, the SEC has devoted significant examination resources to assessing compliance. Examiners review whether performance advertising is fair and balanced, whether testimonials and endorsements comply with disclosure requirements, whether hypothetical performance is appropriately presented and disclaimed, and whether the firm has adopted and implemented written policies reasonably designed to prevent violations.

Best execution. For advisers that direct client trades, examiners evaluate whether the firm is seeking the most favorable terms for client transactions. This includes reviewing the firm's process for evaluating execution quality, any soft dollar arrangements, and the use of affiliated or preferred broker-dealers.

Fees and expenses. Examiners compare the fees actually charged to clients against the fees disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A and advisory agreements. Discrepancies—even unintentional ones caused by system errors or stale disclosures—are treated as potential violations. For private fund advisers, fee and expense allocation practices receive particularly close scrutiny.

Conflicts of interest. The SEC views undisclosed conflicts as a core risk to investors. Examiners will review whether the firm has identified all material conflicts, whether those conflicts are adequately disclosed in Form ADV, and whether the firm has implemented procedures to manage or mitigate them. Common areas of focus include revenue sharing, proprietary products, outside business activities of supervised persons, and allocation of investment opportunities.

After the Examination: Deficiency Letters and Beyond

At the conclusion of the examination, one of several outcomes is possible. In the best case, the staff closes the exam with no findings. More commonly, the firm receives a deficiency letter identifying areas where the firm's practices fell short of regulatory requirements or expectations. A deficiency letter is not a formal enforcement action, but it should not be taken lightly. The SEC expects a substantive written response describing the corrective steps the firm has taken or plans to take.

If the examination reveals more serious issues, the staff may refer the matter to the Division of Enforcement for further investigation. This is a meaningfully different posture—enforcement investigations can result in cease-and-desist proceedings, civil penalties, disgorgement of fees, and industry bars for individuals.

How to Prepare

Preparation for an SEC examination should not begin when the notification letter arrives. It should be an ongoing process embedded in the firm's compliance program.

  • Conduct an annual mock examination. Review your own books and records, marketing materials, fee calculations, and compliance policies with the same rigor an examiner would apply. Identify gaps before the SEC does.
  • Keep your Form ADV current. File amendments promptly when there are material changes. Stale disclosures are a recurring examination finding and an easy one to prevent.
  • Maintain organized records. When the document request arrives, the ability to produce responsive materials promptly signals a well-run compliance program. Delays and disorganization signal the opposite.
  • Train your team. Personnel should understand the firm's compliance policies, know their responsibilities under the code of ethics, and be prepared to speak to examiners clearly and accurately about their roles.
  • Engage counsel early. Having experienced regulatory counsel involved from the outset—particularly during staff interviews and in drafting responses to deficiency letters—can meaningfully affect the trajectory of the examination.

During and After the Examination

During the exam, designate a single point of contact to coordinate with the examination staff. Respond to document requests promptly and completely. Do not volunteer information beyond what is asked, but never withhold responsive documents or mislead examiners—obstruction will transform a routine exam into an enforcement matter.

After receiving the deficiency letter, respond within the requested timeframe with specifics. Vague assurances that the firm will do better are insufficient. The staff expects to see concrete remedial steps: revised policies, corrected disclosures, fee reimbursements where overcharges occurred, and enhanced training. Document everything. The firm's response to a deficiency letter often determines whether the matter is closed or escalated.

An SEC examination is a serious undertaking, but it need not be a threatening one. Firms that invest in compliance infrastructure, maintain accurate records and disclosures, and approach the process with transparency and professionalism consistently achieve better outcomes.
